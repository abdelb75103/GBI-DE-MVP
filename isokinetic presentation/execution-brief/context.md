# Master Planning Brief  -  FIFA Global Burden of Injury and Illness in Football Living Systematic Review

## Conference Talk at Isokinetic 2026

---

## Purpose

This brief sets the narrative, structural, and visual logic for a conference talk on Phase 1 of the FIFA Global Burden of Injury and Illness in Football: the Living Systematic Review. It is meant to guide slide writing, visual design, and narration  -  not to serve as a script.

## Assumptions

- The audience consists of academic and clinically informed sports-medicine professionals.
- Local project documents are the source of truth for scope, emphasis, and boundaries.
- The current export supports directional descriptive signals only and must not be treated as final epidemiological analysis.
- The dashboard is one dissemination output of the living review, not the main subject of the talk.

---

## 1. Core Narrative

### Strongest Narrative Choice

**Football does not only need more player-health data  -  it needs living, comparable, globally inclusive evidence infrastructure.**

This framing works because it gives the audience a clear problem before introducing the project, makes the review feel necessary rather than merely ambitious, positions FIFA GBI as infrastructure rather than a one-off systematic review, and creates room for preliminary descriptive findings without forcing premature claims.

### One-Sentence Thesis

Football is a global sport, but its injury and illness evidence base remains fragmented across disciplines, populations, settings, and reporting standards; the FIFA GBI Living Systematic Review is designed to turn that fragmented literature into a continuously updated, consensus-aligned evidence system.

### Intellectual Arc

1. The problem is global fragmentation
2. The consequence is weak comparability and incomplete burden visibility
3. The solution is a living systematic review built for breadth, rigour, and updating

This arc holds together better than a project-summary talk, a methods-only talk, or a dashboard-led talk would.

---

## 2. Talk Architecture

The talk moves through five narrative beats. Weight distribution below assumes a standard 15-20 minute conference slot.

### Beat 1  -  The Global Game, the Fragmented Evidence (~15%)

Open with the mismatch between the scale of football and the unevenness of its player-health evidence base. Pivot to the surveillance-map paper (McCall et al. 2026) as the clearest "why now" anchor.

Opening ground to cover:

- Football is global; its epidemiology evidence is not yet globally integrated
- Prior reviews have tended to be narrow, static, and weighted toward professional male 11-a-side cohorts
- Meaningful parts of the sport remain underrepresented: futsal, beach soccer, para football, women's football, younger cohorts, non-English literature
- Static reviews age quickly in a fast-moving surveillance field

Draw on two or three directional findings from the surveillance-map paper:

- 49 ongoing national-league surveillance programmes identified across 53 countries
- Concentration in a limited number of countries and in men's competitions
- Illness and mental-health capture less common than injury capture
- Consensus-based methodology not universally adopted

The paper's role here is scene-setting, not detailed review. It shows that the surveillance landscape is real but patchy  -  exactly the conditions under which a living synthesis system becomes valuable.

### Beat 2  -  What FIFA GBI Phase 1 Is (~20%)

Introduce the living systematic review directly. The audience should come away understanding the architecture of a response, not just hearing about another review.

Core messages:

- Covers four football disciplines: association football, futsal, beach soccer, and para football
- Includes all sexes, age groups, competitive levels, and languages
- Encompasses prospective cohort studies and randomised trials reporting injury, illness, or mental health outcomes in competitive football settings
- Designed as living evidence infrastructure with planned annual updates
- Translation pathway: FIFA-hosted dissemination outputs, including a future dashboard and public report aligned with major tournament cycles

What makes this novel is the combination of scope, method, and continuity:

> **FIFA GBI is novel not because it is larger than prior reviews, but because it is designed as living evidence infrastructure for the full ecology of football.**

### Beat 3  -  The Comparability Problem and Consensus Alignment (~20%)

This beat carries the methodological weight of the talk. The point: more studies alone will not solve the problem. Better classification, exposure capture, and reporting discipline matter just as much.

Walk through the consensus lineage:

- **Fuller et al. 2006**  -  established the football-specific baseline for injury definitions, severity, recurrence, and exposure capture
- **Bahr et al. 2020 (IOC consensus / STROBE-SIIS)**  -  widened the lens from narrow injury counts to athlete health problems broadly, with explicit guidance on severity, burden, population description, and reporting standards
- **Waldén et al. 2023**  -  translated the broader IOC framework back into football practice, reinforcing player-level exposure capture and sport-specific diagnostic coding

The audience takeaway here should be clear:

> **A living review only becomes useful if the studies it absorbs can be interpreted through comparable definitions, exposure logic, and burden reporting frameworks.**

Priority emphasis  -  without over-indexing on technical detail:

- Population description: discipline, sex, age, level, and setting
- Exposure definition and measurement
- The injury-versus-burden distinction
- Illness and mental-health visibility
- Football-specific reporting discipline

Comparability should land as a central concern, not a side issue. It is the condition for interpretable synthesis.

### Beat 4  -  Provisional Descriptive Landscape (~25%)

Present early descriptive patterns from the current extraction dataset, framed explicitly as preliminary. These patterns illustrate what the living review is already capturing and where the evidence gaps sit.

**Safe directional signals:**

- **Discipline coverage:** the evidence base is overwhelmingly association football, with much smaller futsal, para-football, and beach-soccer representation
- **Sex distribution:** male-coded studies materially outnumber female-coded studies
- **Level of play:** professional and elite settings dominate; amateur and youth representation is thinner
- **Outcome availability:** injury fields are far more populated than illness fields across the extracted material
- **Temporal distribution:** reasonable representation from recent years (2020-2024), indicating active research production

Frame these as the shape of the evidence base, not final epidemiological results. Even within a large extraction effort, certain populations and outcomes remain underrepresented  -  and a living review is the right instrument for tracking whether those gaps close over time.

**Claims the talk must not make:**

- Final pooled incidence or burden estimates
- Definitive comparisons between football disciplines
- Locked study counts that have not been rechecked after cleaning
- Strong illness or mental-health prevalence conclusions
- Any suggestion that the export equals the final analysis dataset

### Beat 5  -  What Comes Next and the Practical Takeaway (~20%)

Close with forward direction and a practical message.

- The living review will produce annual updates
- A FIFA-hosted dashboard will serve as one dissemination output
- A public report is planned to align with major tournament cycles
- The evidence system is designed for use by clinicians, researchers, and decision-makers

Then land the practical takeaway:

> **If you run a surveillance programme, design a cohort study, or report football injury and illness data  -  consensus-aligned definitions and complete reporting are what allow your work to enter a living evidence system. The field needs your data, but it needs your data to be comparable.**

What the audience can act on:

- Design surveillance studies so they can be compared across contexts
- Report exposure, definitions, burden, and population characteristics clearly
- Resist the assumption that evidence from professional men's 11-a-side football generalises to the whole sport
- Treat living synthesis as part of player-health infrastructure, not an academic abstraction

---

## 3. Handling Provisional Data Without Overclaiming

### Language Register

Hedging language should be consistent throughout. Prefer:

- "Preliminary extraction patterns suggest…"
- "In the current dataset, which remains under cleaning and harmonisation…"
- "Directional signals from the extraction to date indicate…"
- "These patterns will be confirmed against the final analysis dataset"

Avoid:

- "We found that…" (implies completed analysis)
- "The evidence shows…" (implies synthesis-level confidence)
- "X% of studies report…" (implies locked denominators)

### Visual Safeguard

Every results-oriented slide should carry a visual and verbal caution signal:

- Include "Preliminary" in the slide subtitle or a persistent footer label
- Use "current extracted records" rather than "global truth"
- Emphasise distribution, coverage, and completeness rather than synthesis conclusions
- Avoid decimals, confidence language, or phrasing that implies final meta-analysis

Introduce the data-status caveat once at the start of Beat 4. A persistent visual marker then handles ongoing signalling without constant verbal disclaimers that break the flow.

---

## 4. Visual Strategy

### Design Principles

- White or very light background, dark text, FIFA-blue accent colour
- One idea per slide
- Large typography for key claims
- Academic keynote aesthetic  -  clean, not flashy
- No screenshots of the extraction tool or dashboard UI in the main talk body
- All provisional-data visuals carry a "preliminary data" footer label or watermark

### Recommended Visuals by Beat

| Beat | Visual | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1  -  Global game | World map with surveillance programme dots (drawing on McCall et al.) | Show uneven global coverage |
| 1  -  Fragmented evidence | Simple icon grid: injury vs. illness vs. mental health capture | Show asymmetry in what gets measured |
| 2  -  Living review scope | Four-discipline icon set with subgroup dimensions radiating outward | Show that inclusive scope is the novelty |
| 2  -  Living cycle | Circular process diagram: search → extract → validate → update → disseminate | Show recurring infrastructure |
| 3  -  Consensus lineage | Minimal timeline: 2006 → 2020 → 2023 | Show comparability is constructed, not assumed |
| 4  -  Discipline gap | Horizontal bar chart: records per discipline | Make the dominance of association football immediately visible |
| 4  -  Sex gap | Side-by-side proportional area or waffle chart | Show male/female imbalance at a glance |
| 4  -  Outcome gap | Paired bar or dot plot: injury vs. illness field completeness | Make the disparity obvious |
| 5  -  Forward direction | Simple roadmap: annual update cycle → dashboard → public report | Show dissemination follows the science |
| 5  -  Takeaway | Single strong sentence, full-slide typographic treatment | Send the audience home with one idea |

---

## 5. Remotion-Friendly Animation Beats

### Principles

Motion should clarify structure and aid recall, not decorate. Use progressive reveal, grouping and regrouping, and clean tempo shifts between problem, method, and signal slides. Transitions should be smooth and understated  -  horizontal wipes, opacity fades, draw-on effects for charts.

Steer clear of flashy transitions, rapid chart morphs that imply stronger inference than the data supports, and dashboard-like interface animations.

### Recommended Sequences

**Fragmentation to System.** Open with scattered elements representing studies, disciplines, and populations. Animate them into a coherent living-review structure. The message: the project brings order to a fragmented evidence base.

**Global but Uneven.** A map or distributed-node composition. Dots animate in by region, showing concentration rather than universal coverage. The message: football is global, but evidence and surveillance are not evenly distributed.

**Scope Expansion.** Reveal the four football disciplines one by one, then overlay sex, age, level, and language dimensions radiating outward. The message: novelty comes from inclusive scope, not study volume alone.

**Consensus as Backbone.** Step-through animation of three consensus milestones, each node expanding briefly to show its contribution. The message: comparability rests on a deliberate scientific lineage.

**Preliminary Pattern Reveal.** Introduce provisional charts sequentially  -  discipline concentration, sex/setting imbalance, injury-versus-illness reporting asymmetry. Bars and fills draw on to make gaps visible. The message: the current landscape already shows where evidence concentrates and where it thins out.

**Living Update Loop.** Animate the circular process: search → extract → validate → update → disseminate. The message: this is recurring infrastructure, not a static publication.

**End-State Utility.** Dashboard and report outputs appear last, with a quiet sequential fade-in. The message: dissemination follows the science; it does not replace it.

---

## 6. Slide-Level Emphasis

### Non-Negotiable Slides

If the deck has to be short, these moments must survive:

1. The problem slide: global sport, fragmented evidence
2. The "why now" slide anchored to the 2026 surveillance-map paper
3. The "what FIFA GBI is" slide: scope, design, living architecture
4. The comparability and consensus-lineage slide
5. The preliminary landscape slide: discipline, sex, and outcome gaps
6. The living-review workflow slide
7. The practical takeaway slide

### Secondary  -  Include If Time Allows

- Detailed search strategy
- Extraction workflow mechanics in depth
- Dashboard concept visual (a single static frame at most)
- High-profile diagnosis examples (concussion, ACL, hamstring) as illustrative anchors

### Excluded from the Talk Body

- Phase 2 and Phase 3
- Dashboard demonstration or walkthrough
- Extraction tool or software architecture
- Final pooled epidemiological estimates
- Locked study counts or definitive subgroup comparisons

---

## 7. Audience Takeaway  -  What They Should Remember

A conference attendee walking out of this talk should retain three things:

1. **The problem is real and current.** Football epidemiology evidence is fragmented across disciplines, populations, and reporting standards, and existing surveillance is unevenly distributed globally.

2. **FIFA GBI is building a living system, not publishing a single paper.** The living review architecture  -  consensus-aligned extraction, structured quality control, annual updates, accessible dissemination  -  is designed to keep the evidence base current and comparable.

3. **Their own work can contribute.** If they collect or report football health data, consensus-aligned definitions and complete reporting are what allow that work to enter a living evidence system. The field needs comparable data, not just more of it.

## 9. Framing Paper Integration Rules

The McCall et al. 2026 surveillance-map paper should appear in Beat 1 only.

**Do:**

- Cite it as the most current picture of the global surveillance landscape
- Use two or three directional findings (programme count, geographic concentration, injury-vs-illness asymmetry) as framing evidence
- Credit it as motivation for the living review's inclusive scope

**Do not:**

- Reproduce the paper's full results
- Turn Beat 1 into a standalone surveillance-programme review
- Compare the paper's findings directly against the FIFA GBI export

The paper frames the need for the review. It does not replace the review's own epidemiological work.

---

## 10. Open Decisions for the Talk Build Phase

- **Exact slide count and timing allocation.** This brief assumes a 15-20 minute slot but does not lock slide count.
- **Final data figures.** All provisional numbers must be rechecked against the cleaned analysis dataset before the deck is finalised.
- **Co-author and acknowledgment slide content.** Not addressed here.
- **Whether to include a single static dashboard mockup** in the closing beat. The recommendation is to leave it out  -  mention it verbally at most  -  but this is a presenter decision.

---

## Final Recommendation

The talk should be built around a clear scientific claim: football needs living evidence infrastructure because its current player-health evidence is globally important but unevenly captured, unevenly reported, and not yet sufficiently comparable. FIFA GBI should be introduced as the first serious attempt to build that infrastructure across the full football ecosystem.

Preliminary results serve that argument  -  they do not compete with it. They should show that the evidence base is concentrated, incomplete, and structurally uneven. The consensus story explains why that matters scientifically. The dashboard appears late, briefly, and only as proof that this living review is designed for practical use.

Done well, this talk will feel rigorous and timely  -  not because it presents a project, but because it presents a different way of organising football player-health evidence at global scale.
