# Context

## Project Framing

The talk is about the FIFA Global Burden of Injury and Illness in Football project as a Living Systematic Review.

The opening should explain:

- football is global, but the epidemiology evidence base is fragmented
- most previous reviews are narrow, static, and often centred on professional male 11-a-side football
- FIFA GBI is building a living, continuously updated evidence system across football disciplines, sexes, ages, levels of play, and languages

## What Must Be Included

- FIFA GBI project introduction
- why a living systematic review is needed
- novelty of including association football, futsal, beach soccer, and para football
- multilingual and globally inclusive ambition
- the future FIFA-hosted dashboard as one dissemination output
- provisional descriptive findings from the current extraction dataset
- consensus-statement alignment and best-practice reporting logic
- a practical takeaway for the audience

## What Must Not Happen

- do not turn the talk into a dashboard product demo
- do not center Phase 2 or Phase 3
- do not treat the current CSV export as the final cleaned analysis sheet
- do not overclaim pooled epidemiological results from the provisional export

## Data Caveat

The current export is still being cleaned, standardised, and expanded. It is useful for identifying what descriptive signals exist, but any exact counts or claims used in the final talk must later be rechecked against the cleaned analysis dataset.

## Framing Reference

The paper `Player health surveillance in football: a global map of programmes in national and international leagues and tournaments` is a mandatory framing reference. Use it to guide tone, structure, and how global surveillance is described, but do not copy its structure.
```

### `isokinetic presentation/project-notes.md`

# Project Notes

## What the FIFA GBI Living Review Is Trying to Do

- Build a living systematic review of injury and illness in football
- Cover association football, futsal, beach soccer, and para football
- Include all sexes, age groups, levels of play, and languages
- Move beyond static one-off reviews that become outdated quickly
- Support FIFA, clinicians, researchers, and decision-makers with a continuously updated evidence base

## Core Methodological Ideas from the Project Documents

- structured data extraction and quality control
- consensus-aligned coding and reporting
- Bayesian hierarchical synthesis to handle heterogeneity and sparse subgroups
- annual living-review updates
- a FIFA-hosted dashboard as one dissemination route

## Important Talk Implications

- the talk should introduce the living review as infrastructure, not just a paper
- the talk should explain why football epidemiology needs this kind of system
- preliminary findings should be shown as directional outputs of a larger program of work
- comparability and consensus alignment matter, but they should support the main story rather than replace it
```

# Data Status Note

The current export is not the final cleaned analysis sheet.

Use it only to understand:

- what kinds of variables are already being captured
- what descriptive early patterns may be available
- what sorts of subgroup or reporting visuals the talk might support

Do not use it as final truth for:

- locked prevalence or incidence claims
- final pooled results
- definitive counts that have not been rechecked after cleaning and harmonization

The final talk should explicitly mark any results slides as preliminary until the cleaned analysis dataset is confirmed.
