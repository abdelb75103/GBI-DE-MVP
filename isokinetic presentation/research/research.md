# Research Packet

## What To Answer

- What is the strongest conference narrative for the FIFA Global Burden of Injury and Illness in Football Living Systematic Review?
- Which consensus and reporting sources should anchor the methodological story?
- How should the 2026 surveillance-map paper frame the need for FIFA GBI without turning the talk into a separate surveillance-programme review?
- What can the current provisional export support safely, and what should remain explicitly off-limits until the cleaned dataset is confirmed?

## Key Findings

### 1. The strongest framing is "football needs living evidence infrastructure"

The talk should not lead with a dashboard or with a generic project overview. The stronger frame is that football is global, but the systems that generate comparable player-health evidence are still uneven, incomplete, and methodologically inconsistent. FIFA GBI is compelling when presented as infrastructure that connects fragmented surveillance evidence into a living, updateable review across disciplines, sexes, ages, levels of play, and languages.

This framing fits both the local project documents and the 2026 surveillance-map paper. It also gives the audience a reason to care before any provisional results appear.

### 2. The consensus lineage provides the comparability spine

The talk should treat the reporting-consensus literature as the logic that makes a living review scientifically credible.

- The 2006 football consensus established the football-specific baseline for injury definitions, recurrence, severity, and exposure capture. Its enduring value is standardisation at the level of case definition and data collection procedure.
- The 2020 IOC consensus widened the lens from narrow injury counts to athlete health problems more broadly, with explicit guidance on severity, exposure, risk expression, burden, population description, and STROBE-SIIS reporting.
- The 2023 football-specific extension translates that broader framework back into football practice. It reinforces player-level exposure capture, encourages use of sport-specific diagnostic coding systems, and clarifies burden and reporting choices in football settings.

For the talk, the key message is simple: the field does not only need more studies. It needs more studies that can be compared.

### 3. The 2026 surveillance-map paper is the best "why now" framing reference

The surveillance-map paper is useful because it shows that football already has meaningful surveillance activity, but that activity is still unevenly distributed and only partly standardised.

Directional findings from the paper that matter for this talk:

- The study identified responses from 138 national leagues across 53 countries and reported 49 ongoing national-league surveillance programmes.
- It also identified surveillance in 15 international tournaments.
- Ongoing programmes were concentrated in a limited number of countries and were more common in men's competitions than in women's.
- Illness capture and mental-health capture were less common than injury capture.
- Consensus-based methodology was not universal.

That paper should be used as a scene-setting reference, not as a substitute for FIFA GBI. Its role is to show that the surveillance landscape is real but patchy, which strengthens the case for a living, synthesis-focused system.

### 4. Best-practice reporting points that matter most for this audience

For a conference talk, the highest-value methodological points are:

- define the population clearly by discipline, sex, age, level, and setting
- distinguish incidence from burden rather than treating them as interchangeable
- state exactly how exposure was measured
- keep injury, illness, and mental-health surveillance logic visible instead of collapsing everything into one generic outcome bucket
- show that consensus alignment is what makes subgroup comparison and future pooling defensible

This means the talk should avoid implying that "more data" alone solves the problem. Better classification, exposure capture, and reporting discipline are part of the solution.

### 5. The provisional export is useful as a landscape signal, not as final epidemiology

The current export on April 3, 2026 is already useful for planning the talk, but mainly as a picture of the evidence base and the extraction workflow's current reach.

Safe planning signals from the export:

- the dataset is dominated by association-football rows, with much smaller futsal, para-football, and beach-soccer representation
- male-coded and professional or elite settings still dominate the visible extracted material
- injury fields are much more populated than illness fields
- enough structured variables are already being captured to show why subgroup summaries and comparability logic matter

Unsafe uses:

- final pooled injury or illness claims
- definitive discipline comparisons
- locked counts for the final talk deck without rechecking the cleaned dataset

This supports a results section built around provisional descriptive patterns and reporting gaps, not a mature synthesis section.

### 6. The dashboard belongs in the future-facing dissemination lane

The local project documents clearly support mentioning a FIFA-hosted dashboard, but as dissemination infrastructure rather than the star of the talk. The dashboard is evidence of practical intent: the living review is meant to be used. It should appear late in the talk, after the audience understands the evidence problem and the review architecture.

## Risks And Unknowns

- The current export is still being cleaned and harmonized, so any exact figure used in the final talk must be revalidated.
- The surveillance-map paper is about surveillance programmes, not the global burden itself. It frames the need for the review but does not replace the review's epidemiological work.
- Illness and mental-health data appear thinner than injury data both in the surveillance-map paper and in the provisional export, so overbalancing the talk toward those outcomes would be hard to support at this stage.
- The handoff materials are explicit that Phase 2 and Phase 3 should stay out of the main talk body.
- The dashboard can easily crowd out the scientific story if shown too early or too heavily.

## Source List

- [Fuller et al. 2006 football consensus](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/40/3/193) - Foundational football-specific definitions and data-collection procedures for injury surveillance.
- [Bahr et al. 2020 IOC consensus and STROBE-SIIS](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/54/7/372) - The broad reporting and surveillance framework for injury and illness studies in sport.
- [Walden et al. 2023 football-specific IOC extension](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/21/1341) - Football-tailored guidance on exposure, coding, burden, and reporting choices.
- [Ardern et al. 2022 PERSiST guidance](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/56/4/175) - Helpful bridge for systematic-review reporting discipline in sport and exercise contexts.
- [McCall et al. 2026 surveillance-map paper](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/24733938.2026.2642645) - Best framing source for the current global surveillance landscape and its uneven coverage.
