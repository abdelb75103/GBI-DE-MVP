# Master Planning Brief  -  FIFA Global Burden of Injury and Illness in Football Living Systematic Review

## Conference Talk at Isokinetic 2026

---

## 1. Core Narrative

**One-line thesis:** Football is the world's most played sport, but the evidence systems that should protect its players are fragmented, inconsistent, and incomplete  -  FIFA GBI is building the living infrastructure to fix that.

The talk should not open with a project description or a dashboard walkthrough. It should open with a problem the audience already feels: despite decades of football injury research, we still cannot reliably compare findings across disciplines, sexes, ages, or levels of play. The reason is not a lack of studies  -  it is a lack of comparable, continuously synthesized evidence.

FIFA GBI enters the story as the response: a living systematic review that covers association football, futsal, beach soccer, and para football across all populations, built on consensus-aligned definitions and designed to be updated annually rather than published once and left to age.

This framing gives the audience a reason to care before any data appear, and it positions the project as scientific infrastructure rather than a single publication.

---

## 2. Talk Architecture

The talk should move through five narrative beats. Approximate weight distribution is suggested below.

### Beat 1  -  The Global Game, the Fragmented Evidence (~15%)

Open with the scale of football globally, then pivot to the surveillance-map paper (McCall et al. 2026). Use its directional findings to show that surveillance activity exists but is unevenly distributed:

- 49 ongoing national-league surveillance programmes identified across 53 countries
- Concentration in a limited number of countries and in men's competitions
- Illness and mental-health capture less common than injury capture
- Consensus-based methodology not universal

The point is not to review that paper in detail. The point is to show that the landscape is real but patchy, which frames the need for a synthesis layer.

### Beat 2  -  Why a Living Systematic Review (~20%)

Introduce the FIFA GBI project directly. Key messages:

- Covers four football disciplines: association football, futsal, beach soccer, para football
- Includes all sexes, age groups, competitive levels, and languages
- Designed as living evidence infrastructure with planned annual updates
- Not a one-off review that becomes outdated  -  a continuously maintained system

Explain what "living" means concretely: structured extraction, codebook-based quality control, a synthesis framework that can absorb new studies as they appear, and dissemination outputs (including a future FIFA-hosted dashboard and public report) that stay current.

This beat should feel like the audience is seeing the architecture of a solution, not just hearing about another systematic review.

### Beat 3  -  The Comparability Problem and Consensus Alignment (~20%)

This is the methodological spine. The message: more studies alone will not solve the problem. Better classification, exposure capture, and reporting discipline are equally important.

Walk through the consensus lineage briefly:

- **Fuller et al. 2006**  -  established the football-specific baseline for injury definitions, severity, recurrence, and exposure
- **Bahr et al. 2020 (IOC consensus / STROBE-SIIS)**  -  widened the lens to athlete health problems broadly, with explicit guidance on severity, burden, population description, and reporting standards
- **Waldén et al. 2023**  -  translated the broader IOC framework back into football practice, reinforcing player-level exposure capture and sport-specific diagnostic coding

The audience takeaway: FIFA GBI's extraction and coding system is built on this lineage. That is what makes subgroup comparison, future pooling, and cross-study synthesis defensible rather than aspirational.

### Beat 4  -  Provisional Descriptive Landscape (~25%)

Present early descriptive patterns from the current extraction dataset, explicitly framed as preliminary. These patterns illustrate what the living review is already capturing and where the evidence gaps sit.

Safe directional signals to use:

- **Discipline coverage:** the evidence base is overwhelmingly association football, with much smaller futsal, para-football, and beach-soccer representation
- **Sex distribution:** male-coded studies materially outnumber female-coded studies
- **Level of play:** professional and elite settings dominate; amateur and youth representation is thinner
- **Outcome availability:** injury fields are far more populated than illness fields across the extracted material
- **Temporal distribution:** reasonable representation from recent years (2020-2024), indicating active research production

Frame these as the shape of the problem, not as final epidemiological results. The audience should see that even within a large extraction effort, certain populations and outcomes remain underrepresented  -  and that a living review is the right tool for tracking whether those gaps close over time.

**Critical guardrail:** Every results visual must carry a "preliminary  -  subject to final data cleaning" marker. No locked incidence, prevalence, or burden figures. No definitive cross-discipline comparisons. No exact study counts without a note that these will be confirmed against the cleaned analysis dataset.

### Beat 5  -  What Comes Next and the Practical Takeaway (~20%)

Close with forward direction:

- The living review will produce annual updates
- A FIFA-hosted dashboard will serve as one dissemination output, making the evidence base accessible to clinicians, researchers, and decision-makers
- A public report is planned to align with major tournament cycles

Then deliver the practical takeaway for the audience:

> **If you run a surveillance programme, design a cohort study, or report football injury and illness data  -  consensus-aligned definitions and complete reporting are what allow your work to enter a living evidence system. The field needs your data, but it needs your data to be comparable.**

This gives the audience something actionable. It also reinforces the comparability message without turning the close into a methods lecture.

## 3. Visual Strategy and Remotion Animation Beats

The talk should use clean, restrained visuals. No dense tables, no slide clutter, no product-demo screenshots.

### Recommended Visual Types

| Beat | Visual | Animation Note |
|------|--------|----------------|
| 1  -  Global game | World map with surveillance programme dots (inspired by McCall et al.) | Dots animate in by region, showing uneven coverage |
| 1  -  Fragmented evidence | Simple icon grid: injury vs. illness vs. mental health capture rates | Icons fade in to show asymmetry |
| 2  -  Living review scope | Four-discipline icon set (association football, futsal, beach soccer, para football) with subgroup dimensions radiating outward | Radial build animation: disciplines appear, then sex/age/level/language dimensions extend |
| 3  -  Consensus lineage | Minimal timeline: 2006 → 2020 → 2023 | Step-through animation, each node expanding briefly to show its contribution |
| 4  -  Discipline gap | Horizontal bar chart: rows per discipline | Bars draw left to right, making the dominance of association football immediately visible |
| 4  -  Sex gap | Side-by-side proportional area or waffle chart | Animate male vs. female fill to show the imbalance |
| 4  -  Outcome gap | Paired bar or dot plot: injury vs. illness field completeness | Draw simultaneously to make the disparity obvious |
| 5  -  Forward direction | Simple roadmap graphic: annual update cycle → dashboard → public report | Sequential fade-in |

### Design Principles

- White or very light background, dark text, FIFA-blue accent colour
- One idea per slide
- Large typography for key claims
- All provisional-data visuals carry a subtle "preliminary data" watermark or footer label
- No screenshots of the extraction tool or dashboard UI in the main talk body (mention only)
- Remotion transitions should be smooth and understated  -  horizontal wipes, opacity fades, draw-on effects for charts  -  nothing that distracts from the scientific story

## 4. Framing Paper Integration

The McCall et al. 2026 surveillance-map paper should appear in Beat 1 only. Its role is scene-setting: it shows that football has real surveillance infrastructure, but that infrastructure is unevenly distributed and methodologically varied. This creates the logical bridge to "we need a synthesis system."

Do not:

- Reproduce the paper's full results
- Turn Beat 1 into a surveillance-programme review
- Compare the paper's findings directly against the FIFA GBI export

Do:

- Cite it as the most current picture of the global surveillance landscape
- Use two or three of its directional findings (programme count, geographic concentration, injury-vs-illness asymmetry) as framing evidence
- Credit it as motivation for the living review's inclusive scope

---

## 5. How to Handle Provisional Data Without Overclaiming

### Language register

Use hedging language consistently. Preferred phrasings:

- "Preliminary extraction patterns suggest…"
- "In the current dataset, which remains under cleaning and harmonization…"
- "Directional signals from the extraction to date indicate…"
- "These patterns will be confirmed against the final analysis dataset"

Avoid:

- "We found that…" (implies completed analysis)
- "The evidence shows…" (implies synthesis-level confidence)
- "X% of studies report…" (implies locked denominators)

### Structural safeguard

Introduce the data-status caveat once, clearly, at the start of Beat 4. Do not repeat it on every slide  -  a persistent visual marker (footer label or watermark) handles that. This keeps the talk flowing without constant verbal disclaimers.

---

## 6. Audience Takeaway  -  What They Should Remember

A conference attendee walking out of this talk should retain three things:

1. **The problem is real and current.** Football epidemiology evidence is fragmented across disciplines, populations, and reporting standards, and existing surveillance is unevenly distributed globally.

2. **FIFA GBI is building a living system, not publishing a single paper.** The living review architecture  -  consensus-aligned extraction, structured quality control, annual updates, accessible dissemination  -  is designed to keep the evidence base current and comparable.

3. **Their own work can contribute.** If they collect or report football health data, consensus-aligned definitions and complete reporting are what allow that data to enter a living evidence system. The field needs comparable data, not just more data.

---

## 7. Explicit Exclusions

The following are excluded from the talk body by design:

- Phase 2 (direct data collection / surveillance system design)
- Phase 3 (intervention review / prevention evidence)
- Dashboard demonstration or walkthrough
- Extraction tool or software architecture
- Final pooled epidemiological estimates
- Locked study counts or definitive subgroup comparisons

## 9. Open Decisions for the Talk Build Phase

- **Exact slide count and timing allocation.** This brief assumes a standard 15-20 minute conference slot but does not lock slide count.
- **Final data figures.** All provisional numbers must be rechecked against the cleaned analysis dataset before the deck is finalized.
- **Co-author and acknowledgment slide content.** Not addressed in this brief.
- **Whether to include a brief live or animated demonstration of the dashboard concept** in the closing beat. The recommendation is no  -  mention it verbally, show a single static mockup at most  -  but this is a presenter decision.
